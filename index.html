Open Gantry

Welcome to the Open Gantry page.

Open Gantry is a CNC machine designed specifically for cutting wikihouses with a total cost which is less than $400!

Open Gantry is a work in progress! We do not guarantee anything. If you attempt to build this, prepare to spend many hours tweaking, calibrating, and potentially altering the models/design. We can't guarantee you will be able to source all the parts for less than $500. The sub $400 price tag requires the use of a very low quality harbor freight router, which could fail. I personally would use a high quality router. We went to great lengths to be extremely innovative about ways to cut costs. Every idea and design decision was followed by: "how will this effect the cost of the finished product". There are better designs on the market, which higher precision. Your precision will come down to how much work you put into this machine. This is not the most trivial build.

To get started:

$ cd your_workspace
$ git clone https://github.com/google/open-gantry.git

1.) Download all the files

2.) 3d print all of the .stl files
Important: All models are 1:4 scale. However, whether you want a 1:4 scale or 1:1 scale, all 3d printed parts stay the same size except for zgantry-driver.stl which should be scaled to fit your machine.

3.) Using either a laser cutter, CNC, or a lot of patience and a jigsaw cut out all of the svg files
Important: All models are 1:4 scale. If you want a 5ftX9ft CNC you need to scale up all svg files by 400%

4.) Get a bunch of adhesive (CA glue for 1:4, Epoxy for 1:1), and wood screws and start assembling the frame. Whatever you do... make sure the frame is as rigid as possible. A wobbly CNC is a CNC with lots of backlash, and innacurate cuts. Making everything rigid, and eliminating give in the spektra lines are the most important part of this build.

Bill of Materials (work in progress)

  • 1 - 3hp router (or rotary tool for 1:4 scale)
  • 14 - 608 2rs bearings
  • 2 - 5/16 bolts and nuts
  • 12 - small hose clamps
  • 2 - large hose clamps
  • 20 + wood screws (size varies depending on need)
  • 1 - Cheap epoxy or resin (CA glue works for 1:4 scale)
  • 2 - #4 set screw
  • 4 - bearing rollers (sized to fit your rails)
  • 1 - box of bolts & nuts (I like 1/4 20 3" long)
  • ? - 1/4 cold rolled steel rod (1:4 scale) OR 1" galvenized pipe (the rails). Buy as much as you need. Quantity depends on your machines size.
  • 1 - arduino / grbl shield combo pack
  • 1 - power supplyWe are using one with DC 24v 3.75A for 1:4 scale. For 1:1 scale you will need something that can supply at least 12Amp @ 24v (an xbox PSU works well or PC power supply)
  • 1 - 3/8 20 threaded rod (we used 1/4 20 for 1:1)
  • 1 - Drive cables could be 1/16 steel cable or high rating braided spektra fishing line (100-500pound rating for 1:1, 50pound should be fine for 1:4).
  • ? - Enough plywood to do the job. For the best price use 1" OSD sheets. For high quality use better 1" plywood. (for 1:4 scale we used 1/8" plywood which worked great)
  • 3 - Stepper motors Nema 17 for 1:4, The highest torque rating Nema 23~ under 2.9Amp within your budget for 1:1 scale. Sourcing these is tricky. Too much torque and you need more expensive electronics. Too little torque and you will have to make a ton of passes on your cuts or they will simply stall. Price is also a major factor.

Assembly

Cut all the parts out of wood. 3d print all the parts. Look closely at the pictures and try to replicate what we did. Do whatever it takes to make it as rigid as possible.
